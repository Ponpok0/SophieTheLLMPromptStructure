The assumption that urban residents will inevitably find zoos to be “incredibly emotional” because they lack regular contact with nature oversimplifies both human experience and emotional response. While it's true that city life often limits direct exposure to wild environments, the actual emotional impact of visiting a zoo varies widely depending on several factors:

- **Personal values**: Some visitors might feel joy or fascination, especially if they have a deep interest in animals or wildlife. Others might feel discomfort or sadness, particularly if they perceive the animals’ enclosures as restrictive or unnatural.

- **Cultural context**: In some societies, zoos are seen as educational and family-friendly institutions; in others, they are criticized for ethical reasons.

- **Exposure alternatives**: Urban dwellers aren’t completely isolated from nature. Botanical gardens, riverside parks, pet cafes, and even digital nature content can serve as partial substitutes.

- **Zoo design**: Modern zoos vary. Some simulate natural habitats with enrichment programs and conservation efforts, while others remain outdated and barren. Emotional reactions often reflect the perceived welfare of the animals.
